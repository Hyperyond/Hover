# Why Hover drives your real Chrome over CDP instead of a fresh headless context

When you build an AI tool that drives a browser, the easy path is to spin up a fresh headless Chromium for every session. It's clean, isolated, and reproducible. It's also the wrong choice for testing the app you're actively developing. Here's the reasoning behind Hover's architecture.

## The problem with a fresh headless context

A brand-new headless browser starts with nothing: no session, no logged-in state, no localStorage, no cookies. For a marketing page that's fine. For the app you're building — the one behind a login, with a populated cart, a half-filled form, a feature flag toggled on — it's a blank slate that doesn't resemble what you're working on.

So you'd script the login. And the seed data. And the flags. Every session. Just to get back to the state your dev browser was already in. The agent ends up testing a synthetic environment, not your app.

## Hover's approach: connect to a real, already-running Chrome

Hover takes the opposite stance. It connects over the **Chrome DevTools Protocol (CDP)** to a Chrome that's *already running*, and lands on the existing tab whose URL matches your dev-server origin. The agent drives that tab — the real one, with its real state.

This means:

- **You log in once.** The session persists. The agent inherits it.
- **The agent sees your actual app state** — the same DOM, the same cookies, the same localStorage you're looking at.
- **Cross-tab flows work** — popups, payment-provider redirects, new-tab callbacks — because it's a real browser with real tabs, not an isolated context.

The agent never launches its own Chromium. It attaches to what's there and operates within it.

## But not your *primary* Chrome

Here's the important boundary: the Chrome Hover drives is **not** your everyday browser profile. Hover launches an **isolated debug Chrome** under a temporary user-data directory, with remote debugging enabled, navigated to your dev URL. It's a dedicated, persistent profile reused across sessions — separate from the Chrome where you have your email, your banking tabs, and fifty extensions.

Why not just attach to your main browser? Because that would require you to relaunch your everyday Chrome with a remote-debugging port, and it would expose every tab, cookie, and extension on your main session to whatever the agent does. The trade-off Hover makes is honest: you log into your app once inside the debug Chrome, and from then on that profile persists your session across commands and dev-server restarts — without ever putting your primary profile in reach.

## What the widget tells you about all this

Because the widget knows the page it's running in (`window.location.href`) and the service knows which Chrome it can reach over CDP, Hover can answer a question you'd otherwise have to reason about yourself: *is this widget actually in the debug Chrome?*

- **Same window** — origins match; the agent can drive this tab. Normal state.
- **Wrong window** — a debug Chrome exists but you're not in it; one click brings the right tab forward.
- **No debug Chrome** — none is running; one click launches it, navigated to your dev URL.

You never have to think about ports or protocols. The widget guides you to a working state.

## The principle underneath

An agent that tests your app should test *your app* — in the state you're developing it, in a real browser, the way a user would hit it. CDP against a real-but-isolated debug Chrome is how Hover gets there without spawning synthetic environments and without touching the browser you live in.
